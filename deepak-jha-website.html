About

I study how cells control gene expression through chromatin structure — the field broadly known as epigenetics. My work focuses on histone and DNA modifications, transcription factors, and signaling pathways that determine cell identity. I apply these insights to understand disease mechanisms in neurodegeneration, autoimmunity, and cancer.

Before joining industry, I was a post-doctoral fellow with Dr. George Q. Daley at Harvard Medical School and Boston Children's Hospital. I completed my PhD in Biochemistry and Biophysics at the University of North Carolina, Chapel Hill in the lab of Dr. Brian Strahl.

Beyond research, I am engaged in the business of biopharma — venture-backed company creation, venture capital, and business development.

Research Interests
Epigenetics and Chromatin Regulation
How histone and DNA modifications shape gene expression, genome integrity, and cell identity — and how disruptions in these mechanisms drive disease.
Neurodegeneration and Neuroimmunology
The molecular and immune mechanisms underlying Alzheimer's disease and neuroinflammation, and how to identify and validate therapeutic targets in the CNS.
Hematopoiesis and Stem Cell Biology
How blood stem cells form, specify, and commit to lineage — studied through iPSCs, primary cells, and developmental model systems.
Cancer Genomics and Autoimmunity
Mechanisms of cancer predisposition, tumor biology, IBD, and autoimmune disease, with a focus on translating genomic insights into therapeutic strategies.
Human Genetics and Target Discovery
GWAS, human knock-outs, and machine learning methods applied to identifying and validating drug targets across complex diseases.
Biopharma Strategy and Investment
Venture-backed company creation, venture capital, and business development at the interface of science and commercial biopharma.
